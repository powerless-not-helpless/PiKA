## Hiring Brief: Content Strategist

### What this person does

A Content Strategist is responsible for the full lifecycle of written content — from understanding the audience and defining the message architecture, to producing drafts, editing for voice and clarity, and optimizing for distribution. They do not just write; they think about why something is being written, who will read it, what action it should prompt, and how it fits into a larger body of work.

On a practical, day-to-day level, this person would handle:

- Drafting long-form content: articles, essays, reports, thought leadership pieces, newsletters
- Writing short-form and conversion copy: landing pages, email sequences, social posts, CTAs
- Developing and maintaining a consistent voice and tone across all of John's content
- Building and managing a content calendar
- Repurposing existing content across formats (e.g., turning a long article into a thread, a summary, a LinkedIn post)
- Editing and refining drafts produced by other team members or John himself
- Researching topics thoroughly before writing — not just surface-level summaries

### Core skills required

- **Voice and tone mastery** — Able to write in someone else's voice convincingly, not just their own. Adapts register, vocabulary, and rhythm to match the author's identity.
- **Structural thinking** — Knows how to build an argument, a story, or a piece of content with a clear beginning, middle, and end. Understands what information goes where.
- **SEO fundamentals** — Understands how search intent works, how to research keywords, and how to structure content so it ranks without reading like it was written for a machine.
- **Audience analysis** — Can identify who the reader is, what they already know, what they want, and what will make them take action or keep reading.
- **Editing discipline** — Ruthless about cutting what does not serve the piece. Knows the difference between their own attachment to a phrase and whether it actually earns its place.
- **Research** — Comfortable going three or four layers deep on a topic to find the insight that makes a piece stand out from shallow takes.
- **Content architecture** — Can build a content system, not just individual pieces. Understands how a newsletter, a blog, and social presence relate to each other.

### How they think / work style

A great Content Strategist thinks like a reader first and a writer second. Before starting any piece, they ask: What does this person already believe? What do they need to know to trust this? What do I want them to feel when they finish reading?

They are systems thinkers — they do not just want to write today's article, they want to build infrastructure that makes next month's content easier, faster, and more coherent. They keep swipe files. They notice when a pattern is working and when it is not. They read widely, not just in the immediate niche.

Their work style is methodical. They outline before they draft. They draft before they polish. They do not confuse activity with progress — a half-finished well-researched piece is worth more to them than five shallow pieces published on schedule.

They are also communicators, not just writers. They can explain why a piece is structured the way it is, what the editorial decision was, and whether something should be cut or developed.

### What separates good from great

**Good** content strategists produce clean, competent writing that covers the topic. They hit the brief. They meet deadlines. They are reliable.

**Great** content strategists produce pieces that feel inevitable — where you cannot imagine the argument landing any other way. They find the angle no one else has taken on a well-worn topic. They write a headline that stops you. They know when to break the rules of structure because the content demands it.

Specifically, the great ones:

- Have a point of view, not just information
- Write for the one reader, not the average reader
- Understand that distribution is part of the content strategy, not separate from it
- Can tell when a piece is not working and diagnose exactly why, not just feel that something is off
- Produce content that earns trust over time — the reader comes back because this person reliably delivers something worth reading
