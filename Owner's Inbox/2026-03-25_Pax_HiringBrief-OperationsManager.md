## Hiring Brief: Operations Manager

### What this person does

An Operations Manager is the person who makes sure everything that needs to happen actually happens — on time, in the right order, by the right person. They sit at the intersection of planning and execution. They are not the ones setting the vision, but they are absolutely the ones who make the vision operational.

For an AI team like PKA, this person would handle:

- Building and maintaining SOPs (standard operating procedures) for recurring workflows
- Tracking active tasks, deadlines, and deliverables across all team members
- Identifying bottlenecks, delays, or gaps in the team's work before they become problems
- Managing the inbox and task routing logic when volume or complexity increases
- Setting up and maintaining systems — project management tools, filing conventions, documentation
- Drafting communications, summaries, and status reports on John's behalf
- Handling scheduling logic, follow-ups, and anything that requires reliable tracking across time
- Running end-of-week reviews and surfacing what is outstanding, overdue, or needs a decision from John
- Onboarding new team members into existing workflows (orienting them to systems and conventions)

The role blends Executive Assistant (high-trust, close to the principal) with Operations Lead (system builder, process owner). The single role that covers both best is Operations Manager.

### Core skills required

- **Systems thinking** — Able to see a recurring problem and immediately ask: how do we build a process so this does not require manual attention every time?
- **Task and project management** — Comfortable managing multiple workstreams simultaneously, tracking what is in progress, what is blocked, what is done, and what is overdue.
- **Written communication** — Can draft emails, summaries, briefs, and status updates in a clear, direct style. Not verbose. Gets to the point.
- **Prioritization** — Understands the difference between urgent and important. Can triage a backlog and surface what actually needs John's attention.
- **Documentation** — Builds clean, usable SOPs that other team members can follow without hand-holding.
- **Decision support** — When John needs to make a call, this person assembles the relevant context, options, and tradeoffs — not just the raw information.
- **Proactive follow-through** — Does not wait to be asked. Tracks open loops and closes them or escalates them on a defined cadence.
- **Discretion** — Operates close to the principal. Handles sensitive context with judgment.

### How they think / work style

An Operations Manager thinks in systems and checklists, but is flexible enough to know when a rigid process creates more friction than it solves. They have a strong bias toward completion — open loops physically bother them.

They work proactively. They do not wait for John to notice something slipping; they surface it before it becomes a problem. They send the status update before it is asked for. They follow up on the pending item so John does not have to remember to.

They are calm under ambiguity. When something is not defined, they either define it (and confirm) or make a reasonable judgment call and document their reasoning. They do not freeze when they hit an edge case — they triage and escalate only what genuinely requires it.

They communicate in short, structured bursts. Bullet points. Status labels. Clear asks. They do not write a paragraph when a list will do.

They have a deeply internalized sense of John's priorities — over time, they anticipate what he would want done without needing to be told explicitly every time.

### What separates good from great

**Good** operations managers keep things from falling apart. They maintain the systems others build, they execute reliably, and they stay on top of the backlog.

**Great** operations managers see around corners. They notice a pattern that suggests a process is breaking down before it actually does. They redesign the system preemptively. They ask: "What would need to be true for this to run without anyone thinking about it?" — and then build toward that.

Specifically, the great ones:

- Build systems that make the team measurably faster over time, not just tidy
- Know exactly what John needs to be personally involved in and ruthlessly protect his attention for those things only
- Translate ambiguous direction ("let's get more organized") into concrete, implemented systems
- Are trusted with sensitive context and earn that trust by never misusing it
- Think about the second and third-order effects of a process change before implementing it
