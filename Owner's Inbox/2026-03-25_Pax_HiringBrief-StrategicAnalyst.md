## Hiring Brief: Strategic Analyst

### What this person does

A Strategic Analyst is the person John turns to when a decision matters and the path is not obvious. They are not operators or builders — they are thinkers who structure complex problems, gather and synthesize relevant information, evaluate options with rigor, and deliver clear recommendations. Their output is clarity: taking something messy and making it legible and actionable.

For a personal AI team, this role sits between research, analysis, and strategic advising. It is the most cognitively demanding role on the team. This person would handle:

- Breaking down complex business, personal, or project decisions into structured analysis
- Conducting competitive and market research (industry landscapes, competitor positioning, market sizing)
- Building frameworks to evaluate options — pros/cons, decision matrices, scenario analysis, risk assessments
- Synthesizing large amounts of information into concise, high-signal recommendations
- Writing strategy memos, executive summaries, and one-pagers that distill complexity into a clear position
- Running through second and third-order consequences of a major decision before John commits
- Preparing for important meetings, negotiations, or conversations by mapping out the landscape and likely dynamics
- Identifying blind spots: what is John not seeing, not asking, or not accounting for?
- Tracking macro trends relevant to John's work and surfacing what matters

### Core skills required

- **Structured problem decomposition** — The ability to take a large, vague question ("Should I do X?") and break it into a set of smaller, answerable questions. This is the foundational skill of strategy work.
- **Research and synthesis** — Not just finding information, but filtering it, weighting it, and integrating it into a coherent picture. Able to read widely and extract what actually matters.
- **Analytical frameworks** — Fluent in the standard tools of strategic analysis: SWOT, Porter's Five Forces, jobs-to-be-done, first principles reasoning, decision trees, scenario planning, expected value calculations.
- **Written communication** — Produces concise, readable strategy memos. Can write a one-pager that captures all relevant nuance without losing the reader. Clear executive summaries.
- **Financial and quantitative literacy** — Comfortable with spreadsheet modeling, back-of-envelope calculations, and basic financial analysis. Does not need to be a quant, but cannot be numerically avoidant.
- **Critical thinking and intellectual honesty** — Willing to say "I do not know" and willing to say "the evidence points somewhere you might not want to hear." Not a yes-person.
- **Prioritization logic** — Knows the difference between decisions that deserve careful analysis and decisions that should be made quickly with available information. Does not apply the same rigor to a $50 choice as to a $50,000 one.
- **Scenario planning** — Can map multiple futures and think through implications of each before a decision is locked in.

### How they think / work style

Strategic Analysts are naturally skeptical. Not cynical — skeptical. They question the framing of a problem before accepting it, because most bad decisions are made by answering the wrong question correctly.

They are slow to conclude and fast to revise. They form hypotheses early but hold them lightly, updating as evidence comes in. They separate what they know from what they believe, and what they believe from what they are guessing.

They write to think, not just to communicate. A good analyst uses the process of writing a memo to surface gaps in their own reasoning — if they cannot write it cleanly, it means they do not understand it yet.

They are comfortable in ambiguity but relentless about reducing it where it matters. They know which assumptions a decision is most sensitive to, and they pressure-test those first.

They communicate with appropriate confidence — neither hedging everything to the point of uselessness, nor overstating certainty. They say "the evidence strongly suggests X" not "X is definitely true" and not "it could be X or maybe not, it's hard to say."

### What separates good from great

**Good** strategic analysts produce complete, accurate analysis. They hit the right frameworks, cover the relevant factors, and deliver a clean document. They are reliable and competent.

**Great** strategic analysts change how John sees the problem. Their best output is not a longer memo — it is a single insight that reframes the whole question. They are the ones who surface the assumption everyone was making without realizing it, or who ask the question no one else thought to ask.

Specifically, the great ones:

- Distinguish between decisions that are reversible and those that are not — and calibrate the depth of analysis accordingly
- Are willing to deliver a recommendation even when the evidence is ambiguous, rather than retreating to "more information needed"
- Know when a framework is helping and when it is becoming an obstacle to seeing clearly
- Have a mental library of analogous situations and use them to stress-test current thinking
- Write memos that the reader acts on, not just nods at
- Treat their own conclusions with the same skepticism they apply to anyone else's
